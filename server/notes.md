## experience at Warner Bros Discovery
When I was a kid, I watched Cartoon Network and HBO almost every day. Back then, Warner Bros. Discovery was just the company behind the shows I grew up with. I never imagined that one day I'd walk into the same company as a Software Engineering Intern.

On 29th September 2025, I joined the Bleacher Report team at WBD. It was my first on-site internship, and to be honest, I was more nervous than confident.

I came from a frontend background, while the team I joined was completely backend. GraphQL, production services, architecture discussions, distributed systems... everything felt new.

The first few weeks were all about KT sessions and understanding a codebase that was much larger than anything I'd worked on before. Then my first task arrived.

I expected something small.

Instead, I was asked to own the POC for migrating a legacy JSON Blob payload to GraphQL.

That meant first understanding why the migration was even needed. I learned GraphQL from scratch, went through thousands of lines of payloads across different sports, spoke with frontend engineers to understand which fields actually mattered, and eventually designed the migration approach.

Somewhere during that process, I realized I had stopped thinking, "Backend is difficult." Instead, I had started enjoying it.

One of the biggest moments for me came when I had to author the LADR for the migration and present it to engineers and leadership across multiple teams.

I still remember standing there thinking,

"I'm just an intern... am I really supposed to present this?"

I was nervous. My hands were literally shaking.

But my managers and mentors trusted me enough to let me lead that discussion, answer questions, defend design decisions, and eventually get the proposal approved across multiple stakeholders.

That confidence stayed with me for the rest of my internship.

After that, every project felt bigger than the previous one.

I designed Datadog dashboards, documented service flows, worked on mobile testing, solved production bugs, and gradually started collaborating with teams outside my own.

One project that I'll probably remember for a long time was building an AI-powered Mock Server.

It started as research.

I spent days comparing open-source tools before settling on Pact and Microcks for bidirectional contract testing. Then came building the mock server itself using Express.js, Microcks, Groq and Ollama.

That project also taught me something no tutorial ever could.

Some nights stretched well past 2 AM because the AI simply refused to behave the way I wanted it to. I kept rewriting prompts, changing approaches, testing scenarios, and talking to developers across teams to understand what real-world use cases they actually needed.

Looking back, those late nights were frustrating while they were happening, but they're also some of the memories I'll probably smile about years from now.

The best part of this internship wasn't a particular project.

It was realizing how much engineering is about people.

Working with frontend engineers, backend engineers, AI teams, Global Quality Engineering, presenting ideas, asking questions that sometimes felt silly, defending design decisions, and learning from people with years of experience.

Every conversation taught me something.

As I write this, my last day is still a week away.

I've already received the mail about returning my laptop, and it honestly feels strange. It feels like I joined just yesterday.

I'm sure the coming week is going to be emotional.

Whatever happens next, I'm leaving with something much more valuable than just technical experience.

I leave knowing that I can walk into an unfamiliar problem, learn what I need to learn, ask for help when I need it, and eventually build something meaningful.

Thank you to everyone who made these last ten months unforgettable—Nathaniel D. Latoni, Archit, Priyadarshini Chavan, John Paul Chandy, Naman, and every engineer who answered my endless questions, reviewed my code, challenged my ideas, and trusted an intern with responsibilities I never expected to receive.

Thank you, Warner Bros. Discovery.

Here's to whatever comes next.
